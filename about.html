What I believe about design

Design is only good if it
actually helps someone.

I got into design because I couldn't stop noticing when things were unnecessarily hard to use. A confusing form. An unintuitive flow. A label that means something to the engineer who wrote it and nothing to anyone else. These things add up to friction — and friction has real costs for real people.

The thing I care most about is the research. I find that if you spend enough time with real users, asking real questions and actually listening to the answers, the right design almost always reveals itself. The skill is in running good sessions, spotting the patterns, and then having the nerve to simplify.

I'm also genuinely interested in design as a business tool. Good UX isn't a nice-to-have — it's directly connected to retention, conversion, support costs, and trust. I try to make that case clearly and consistently wherever I work.

The areas I'm drawn to professionally are the ones where design can make a measurable positive difference: sustainability, health, financial inclusion, and education. Not because I'm naive about commercial constraints, but because I think purposeful work and successful products aren't in conflict — they reinforce each other.

01

Research first

I don't sketch until I understand what's actually wrong. User interviews, diary studies, card sorting — before any wireframes.

02

Honest over optimistic

Designing around uncomfortable truths makes worse products. I'd rather surface the real problem than paper over it.

03

Ship, then improve

A bias toward getting things in front of real users. Iteration with real data beats perfecting in isolation every time.

04

Design for trust

The most durable design outcomes are built on user trust. I treat it as the most valuable thing a product can accumulate.

The personal bit

A bit about who
I actually am.

I'm originally from London, now based in Berlin — a city that suits me well. I'm curious about most things, which helps with design work: the faster you can genuinely care about a new problem domain, the faster you can do good work in it.

Outside of work I follow the things most designers follow — good typography, interesting spatial design, photography — but I'm also fairly obsessive about cooking, and I believe strongly that walking around a city you don't know is one of the best forms of research available.

I'm open to full-time roles and occasionally the right freelance project. If what you're building sounds interesting, I'd like to hear about it.
