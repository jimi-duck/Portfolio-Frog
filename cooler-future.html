The brief

Sustainable investing existed.
Sustainable investing you could
actually trust — didn't.

Green investing had a credibility problem. It's easy to call a fund "sustainable"; it's much harder to stand behind that when someone actually scrutinises it. Most products hid behind jargon or leaned on vague impact claims that didn't survive a second look.

Cooler Future took the opposite line and made transparency the whole strategy: show people exactly what's in their portfolio, what it excludes, how the credentials are verified, and what their money actually does — including the parts that aren't flattering.

I joined before anything had been designed. The remit was broad — the mobile app, the marketing website, and the design system holding them together — but the first real job was understanding who we were building for, and what each of them needed the product to do.

Research came first, and it shaped everything that followed.

Research — understanding both audiences

Two very different users,
both looking at the same screen.

Interviews, diary studies and card sorting across both segments surfaced something the brief had glossed over: we weren't building for one kind of person with one kind of need. We were building for two, and they'd look at the same screen and ask entirely different questions.

Understanding both of them — what they already knew, what they were afraid of, what would build trust and what would break it — became the foundation for everything else. The research didn't just inform the design; it reframed what the product needed to be.

First-time investors

Curious but overwhelmed

Interested, vaguely guilty, not equipped to act. The question they arrive with: "Is this actually better than just leaving my money in the bank?" The answer had to be woven into the product — not a separate explainer.

Existing investors

Skeptical and evidence-hungry

Already investing. Already burned by greenwash. They arrive asking one thing: "Prove this is actually different from what I've already got." For this audience, transparency wasn't just a value — it was the only thing that would win them over.

Onboarding — removing friction without removing rigour

Opening a regulated investment
account is legally complex.
It doesn't have to feel it.

New users had to open a German depot account as part of signup — KYC, identity verification, tax information, regulatory disclosures. This wasn't optional and it couldn't be hidden. The job was to make it feel like the minimum necessary friction, and nothing more.

The approach was methodical: anything that could be deferred was deferred. Anything legally skippable was removed. Every anxiety-inducing step was front-loaded with context — users knew what they'd need before they needed it. Progress was always visible. Nothing appeared without explanation.

Once registered, users moved straight into fund selection — choosing the themes that matched their values before committing any money. Smart Energy, Clean Water, Circular Economy, Forestry & Timber. The investment followed the conviction.

For a product like this, onboarding completion is the metric that matters most. Every unexpected point of friction is somewhere people drop off. Getting it right wasn't a nice-to-have — it was the difference between a business that worked and one that didn't.

The portfolio experience

Clean enough to trust
at a glance. Deep enough
to explore.

The portfolio screen was the product's home — the place users returned to most often. It had to answer a simple question immediately: how is my money doing? But it also had to connect that financial performance to actual environmental impact, honestly rather than decoratively.

Fund detail screens gave the skeptical investor what they needed: performance data, a sector breakdown, and a clear account of what the fund excludes and why. The transparency that research told us would build trust was designed into every layer of the experience.

The impact problem

Showing what money does.
Harder than it sounds.

Showing the environmental impact of someone's investments was the hardest problem on the product. Regulators, data providers and environmental accounting standards all disagreed on what could be shown and how.

The causal chain between a €500 investment and a wind farm involves enough intermediaries to make clean attribution near-impossible. I explored several directions — goal-based breakdowns, supported project feeds, carbon dashboards with real-world equivalents. Each revealed something new about where the line between useful and misleading sat.

This stayed exploratory work. The value was in setting an honest principle to build from: show what the data actually supports, give it clear context, and never overclaim.

Education — trust built through knowledge

Being honest was
how we won people over.

For both types of user, trust was the barrier — and trust can't be declared, only earned. So the Learn section was built to be a real resource: the fundamentals for beginners, and proper fund methodology for the more sceptical, experienced investor.

Courses moved at the user's own pace, tracked progress, and let people suggest topics we hadn't covered yet. It wasn't marketing dressed up as education — it was the product doing exactly what it promised: making sustainable investing legible to anyone.

Marketing website

Two surfaces.
One world.

Alongside the mobile app, I was designing the marketing website — often the first place a potential user encountered Cooler Future. The visual language, tone, and hierarchy of trust signals had to feel completely cohesive with the app. For a product where credibility was everything, a disconnect between the two would have been costly. The design system I built was the thread connecting both.
